Kinetic vs Static Friction

Find a large, heavy piece of furniture. Apply a steady, horizontal push to the furniture. Increase your force until the furniture starts to move. Push just enough to keep the furniture moving. Did you apply the same amount of force? Was it easier to get the furniture to start moving or easier to keep the furniture moving?

Hopefully you noticed that you had to apply a larger force to get the furniture to start moving. Once the furniture was moving, you could apply less force to keep it moving. This is because there are two types of friction: static and kinetic friction.

Static friction is the friction that an object experiences when it is at rest. Static friction is actually a range of friction forces. When you applied a small force to your furniture, the furniture did not move because your force was canceled by the static friction force (Newton’s First Law). If you increase your applied force, the static friction force increases to match. It’s not until you apply a force that exceeds the maximum static friction force that the furniture starts to move.

Kinetic friction is the friction an object experiences while in motion. As noted above, it is almost always smaller than the maximum static friction force.

What determines the amount of friction?

Rub your hands together while pressing very lightly. Then rub your hands together while pushing them together with a lot of force. What do you notice? The amount of force pressing the two objects together is one factor in determining the amount of friction. This force is the normal force.

Think about it this way: when dragging heavy objects across a carpeted surface, it is often easier to lift up slightly on the object while dragging. Why is this? By holding some of the object’s weight, you are decreasing the normal force between the object and the ground, which then causes a decrease in friction and makes it easier to drag the object.

The other factor that will determine the magnitude of a friction force is the relative roughness of the two surfaces that are rubbing against each other. Run your hand against a smooth desk and then run your hand against sandpaper. Even with the same normal force, you’ll notice a difference in friction. The relative roughness of the two surfaces is given by a constant called the coefficient of friction (\[\mu_s\] for static friction, \[\mu_k\] for kinetic friction). It is an experimentally determined ratio of forces (friction and normal), which means that it has no units.

The greater the roughness of both materials, the larger the coefficient of friction. That said, most coefficients have values that are less than 1.

Friction Equation

When all of these factors are combined, friction is:

\[ F_f = \mu F_N \]

Where \[F_f\] is the friction force (either maximum static or kinetic), \[\mu\] is the coefficient of friction (either static or kinetic), and \[F_N\] is the normal force. This is one of the easiest equations to remember: friction is “fun” or “\[f = \mu N\]”.

Note: This equation only indicates the magnitude (number value) of the friction force. You will need to determine the direction of the force from your force vector diagram.

Example 1

A student applies a horizontal 20.0 N force to a 4.0 kg box. The coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.4. What is the acceleration of the box?

Show/Hide Answer
Answer:

Use Newton’s First Law in the vertical direction to determine the normal force (the normal force cancels with the weight of the object). Then solve for the friction force.

Use Newton’s Second Law in the horizontal direction:
\[ \Sigma F_x = ma \]
\[ f + F_{student} = ma \]
\[ (-15.7\,N) + (20.0\,N) = (4.0\,kg)(a) \]
\[ a = 1.1\,m/s^2 \]

Example 2 (with angles)

A student can apply up to 200 N force to a 15.0 kg box at an angle 30° below the horizontal before the box starts to move. What is the coefficient of static friction?

Show/Hide Answer
Answer:
\[ W = mg \]
\[ W = (15.0\,kg)(9.8\,N/kg) = -147\,N \]
\[ F_x = (200\,N)\cos 30^\circ = 173\,N \]
\[ F_y = (200\,N)\sin 30^\circ = -100\,N \]
Vertical forces cancel (Newton’s First Law):
\[ \Sigma F_y = 0 \]
\[ N + F_y + W = 0 \]
\[ N + (-100\,N) + (-147\,N) = 0 \]
\[ N = 247\,N \]
Horizontal forces cancel:
ΣFx=0 \Sigma F_x = 0
\[ f + F_x = 0 \]
\[ f + (173\,N) = 0 \]
\[ f = -173\,N \]
Solve for the coefficient of static friction:
\[ f = \mu_s N \]
\[ 173\,N = \mu_s (247\,N) \]
\[ \mu_s = 0.7 \]
