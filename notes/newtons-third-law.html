Newton's Third Law

Newton’s Third Law is often misunderstood. It does not describe the motion of an object (that’s First and Second Law). Instead, it tells us:

“Every force has an equal-sized but opposite direction force.”

Or, more precisely: Any two objects that push or pull on each other will always push or pull with equal amounts of force in opposite directions, regardless of motion. These are called reaction pairs.

Example 1: Falling Ball
Drop a ball from some height (ignore air resistance). What are the forces acting on the ball as it falls?
Show/Hide Answer

The only force on the ball is the downward pull of the Earth (weight).

What is the Newton’s Third Law reaction pair?
The ball pulls up on the Earth with the same amount of force. We don’t see this on the ball’s force diagram because it’s a force on the Earth.

Why doesn’t the Earth move?
It does, but the effect is negligible due to the Earth’s huge mass.

\[ \Sigma F = ma \]
The ball’s acceleration is large (small mass), the Earth’s acceleration is tiny (huge mass).

Example 2: Horse & Sled
A horse applies a constant, horizontal pulling force on a heavy sled across a flat surface. Both the horse and the sled accelerate. Compare the force of the horse on the sled to the force of the sled on the horse.
Show/Hide Answer

Newton’s Third Law: \[ F_{\text{horse on sled}} = -F_{\text{sled on horse}} \]
The forces are equal in size and opposite in direction, regardless of motion.

How can the sled accelerate if the forces are equal?
To determine motion, consider all forces on each object. The sled accelerates because the horse’s force is greater than friction on the sled. The horse accelerates because friction on the horse is greater than the sled’s pull.

Example 3: Student & Car
A student tries to push a car up a hill, but both move down the hill instead. Which statement is most accurate?
  1. The force of the car on the student is larger than the force of the student on the car
  2. The force of the car on the student is smaller than the force of the student on the car
  3. The force of the car on the student is equal to the force of the student on the car
Show/Hide Answer

Correct answer: (3)
Any two objects pushing or pulling each other will push/pull with the same amount of force in the opposite direction, regardless of motion. These are reaction pairs (Newton’s Third Law).

Example 4: Box & Wall
A student pushes a box against a wall. Identify all of the Newton’s Third Law reaction pair forces.
Show/Hide Answer
  • Normal force of wall on box = force of box on wall (opposite direction)
  • Force of Earth on box = force of box on Earth (opposite direction)
  • Normal force of ground on box = force of box on ground (opposite direction)
  • Force of student on box = force of box on student (opposite direction)
