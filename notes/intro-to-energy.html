Introduction to Energy

The official definition of energy is that energy is the ability to do work. Which begs the question: what is the scientific definition of work? Unfortunately, the scientific definition of work involves the term "energy" which means we've got a confusing circular set of definitions. A more practical definition of energy is that energy is a substance-like quantity that can be transferred between objects and is responsible for all system changes.

Helpful Analogies

Energy is like information: It doesn't have mass and it doesn't take up space but it can be stored and transferred. The method in which information is stored will determine how that information can be used.

Energy is like money in a bank: There are different types of accounts (savings, checking, business, etc). Money can be transferred between accounts and there are specific rules about how money stored in those accounts can be used.

Mechanical Ways to Store Energy

There are three main mechanical methods of energy storage:

Kinetic Energy (K)

Related to the motion of the object. The more kinetic energy an object stores, the faster that object can move. Also related to mass - it takes more energy to move larger masses.

Gravitational Potential (Ug)

Related to the object's height in a gravitational field. The higher up an object is, the more gravitational potential energy is stored. Also related to mass.

Spring/Elastic Potential (Us)

Related to the amount of stretch or compression experienced by an elastic material. The further stretched or compressed, the more energy stored. Related to material elasticity.

Energy Bar Graphs (LOL Graphs)

An energy bar graph is a qualitative way to keep track of how a system stores energy and account for energy changes. It shows how the system stores energy before and after any system changes occur, as well as any energy transferred in or out of the system during the change.

Important: Energy is conserved - it cannot be created or destroyed. The bar graphs can be used to set up a conservation of energy relationship.

Practice Examples

Example 1: Spring Box Launch

A box is pressed down against a large spring. The spring is released and the box is projected upward to some maximum height.

Conservation of energy equation:

\[U_s = U_g\]

Example 2: Car Crash

A moving car crashes into a wall and comes to a stop.

Conservation of energy equation:

\[K - W = 0\]

Example 3: Bungee Jump

A bungee jumper stands on a tall platform. The student jumps off the platform and falls until the bungee cord is fully stretched out with the student several feet about the ground.

Conservation of energy equation:

\[U_g = U_s + U_g\]

Example 4: Box on Rough Surface

A box moving across a rough surface slows due to friction.

Conservation of energy equations:

Option 1: Kstart=Kend+UintK_{start} = K_{end} + U_{int}

Option 2: Kstart−W=KendK_{start} - W = K_{end}
