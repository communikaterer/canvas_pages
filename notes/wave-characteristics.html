Parts of a Wave

A wave is a way that oscillations transport energy from one point to another.

Types of waves:

  • Transverse: Oscillation is perpendicular to wave direction (e.g., light, water waves, strings).
  • Longitudinal: Oscillation is parallel to wave direction (e.g., sound, earthquake waves).

In a transverse wave, particles move up and down while the wave moves horizontally.
Key parts:
- Crest: Top of the wave
- Trough: Bottom of the wave
- Wavelength (λ): Distance for one full cycle (crest to crest or trough to trough)
- Amplitude: Height from equilibrium to crest/trough

Period (T): Time for one wave to pass a point
Frequency (f): Number of waves per second (Hz)

In a longitudinal wave, oscillation is parallel to the wave direction.

Examples: sound, earthquake waves, ocean tides.
Key parts:
- Compression: Regions where particles are close together
- Rarefaction: Regions where particles are spread out
- Wavelength: Distance between compressions or rarefactions
- Amplitude: Maximum displacement from equilibrium

The symbol for wavelength is the Greek letter lambda: λ (measured in meters).

Period vs Frequency

Frequency (f): Number of oscillations or waves per second (Hz).
Period (T): Time for one oscillation or wave (seconds).

The relationship:
\[ f = \frac{1}{T} \qquad T = \frac{1}{f} \]

Show/Hide Example

Example: A student moves her hand up and down 20 times in 5 seconds.
Frequency: \[ f = \frac{20}{5} = 4\,\text{Hz} \] Period: \[ T = \frac{1}{4} = 0.25\,\text{s} \]

Frequency vs Wavelength

If a wave source oscillates faster (higher frequency), more waves are created per second and the wavelength is shorter.

If the source oscillates slower (lower frequency), fewer waves are created per second and the wavelength is longer.

As frequency increases, wavelength decreases (inverse relationship).
The exact inverse of frequency is period, not wavelength.
