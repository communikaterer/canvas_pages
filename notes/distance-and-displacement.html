Scalars and Vectors in Motion

Scalar Quantities

A scalar measurement is any measurement that includes ONLY a magnitude (a number value).

Examples include: distance, speed, time, mass, volume, etc.

Vector Quantities

A vector measurement is any measurement that includes both a magnitude (a number value) and a direction.

Examples include: displacement and velocity.

Distance vs. Displacement

Distance

Is equal to the total amount of travel, regardless of the direction

Displacement

Is the difference between the final position and the starting position

Speed vs. Velocity

Speed

Describes how fast an object moves

Velocity

Describes how fast AND the direction of movement

Example 1: Basic Motion

A person moves forwards 10 m and then backwards 4 m. What is the displacement and the distance?

Show Solution

Distance: The direction doesn't matter - just add up all the traveling that occurred.

Distance = 10 + 4 = 14 m

Displacement: The direction DOES matter. Forward motion is positive and backward motion is negative.

Displacement = (+10 m) + (-4 m) = 6 m, to the right

In other words, the person ended 6 m in front of their starting position.

Using Velocity Graphs

Important Note:

  • Scientists refer to any line on a graph as a "curve" even if it's straight
  • "Area under the curve" means the area between the line and the horizontal axis
  • For lines in the fourth quadrant, this means finding the area ABOVE the line

Here is the velocity graph for an object that started at a position of 5 m and then moved forwards at a rate of 3 meters every second.

The area takes the shape of a rectangle:

\[ A = \text{base} \times \text{height} \]

\[ A = (6 \text{ s})(3 \text{ m/s}) \]

\[ A = 18 \text{ m} \]

This is both the distance and displacement of the object!

Here's a velocity graph for an object that moved left at 5 m/s for 2 seconds, then right at 5 m/s for 4 seconds:

Finding the total area with two rectangles:

\[ A = (2 \text{ s})(-3 \text{ m/s}) + (4 \text{ s})(+3 \text{ m/s}) \]

\[ A = (-6 \text{ m}) + (12 \text{ m}) \]

\[ A = 6 \text{ m} \]

This is the displacement - the object ends up 6 m past its starting point.

Fun Fact: The physics symbol for horizontal displacement is \(\Delta x\) where:

  • \(\Delta\) (delta) represents "final - initial"
  • \(\Delta x\) means "displacement = final position - initial position"

Key Points:

  • Distance = total area under velocity curve (ignore negative values)
  • Displacement = total area under velocity curve (include negative values)
  • Velocity graphs don't show starting position
  • If no starting position is given, assume zero

Show Solution for Part A

The object:

  • Moves forward at 5 meters/second for 6 seconds
  • Then moves backwards at 2 meters/second for 4 seconds

(Remember: +velocity = forwards, -velocity = backwards)

Show Solution for Part B

Displacement:

\[ \Delta x = (6 \text{ s})(+5 \text{ m/s}) + (4 \text{ s})(-2 \text{ m/s}) \]

\[ \Delta x = (+30 \text{ m}) + (-8 \text{ m}) \]

\[ \Delta x = 22 \text{ m} \]

Distance:

\[ d = (6 \text{ s})(5 \text{ m/s}) + (4 \text{ s})(2 \text{ m/s}) \]

\[ d = (30 \text{ m}) + (8 \text{ m}) \]

\[ d = 38 \text{ m} \]

Show Solution for Part C

Since no starting position was given, we'll assume a starting position of zero.

  • First 6 seconds: slope = +5 m/s
  • Next 4 seconds: slope = -2 m/s

Remember: slope of a position vs time graph = velocity!
