Units of Measurement (SI Units)

All SI (System International, aka the metric system) units of measurement boil down to various combinations of seven basic units: seconds, kilograms, meters, kelvin, ampere, moles, and candela.

For example, a unit of pressure you may be familiar with is kPa (kilopascals). A pascal is really just a quick way of writing: kg / (s²·m).

How are these units defined? The definition of what constitutes "one" of each type of measurement has changed several times over the years!

  • Second: In 1960, defined as 1/31,556,925.9747 of the year 1900. In 1967, redefined based on vibrations of a cesium atom. Updated again in 2019 for more specificity.
  • Kilogram: For a long time, defined by a platinum-iridium alloy chunk in a vault. In 2018, redefined based on Planck's constant.

These changing definitions don't affect everyday measurements, but they matter for scientists working in high-precision fields.

Unit Conversions

Why are there so many different ways to measure the same thing? Partly for ease of reporting and partly because different countries and organizations developed their own systems.

Scientists must be able to switch between different measuring units. Even professionals can make mistakes: in 1999, NASA lost its $125 million Mars Climate Orbiter due to a failure to convert between metric and English measurement systems.

Non-Metric Unit Conversions

Non-metric conversions are more involved than just moving a decimal. We'll use the fraction method (dimensional analysis).

Show/Hide Example: Miles to Feet

Convert 0.05 miles to feet (5280 ft = 1 mile):
0.05 miles × (5280 ft / 1 mile) = 264 ft

Show/Hide Example: Miles to Meters

Convert 0.050 miles to meters (1.00 m = 3.28 ft):
0.050 miles × (5280 ft / 1 mile) × (1 m / 3.28 ft) ≈ 80.5 m

You can chain several conversions together using this method.

Density as a Conversion Factor

Density shows the relationship between an object's mass and volume. For example, if a material has a density of 8.0 g/mL, there are 8.0 grams in every 1 mL of volume.

Show/Hide Example: Mass to Volume

Given 3.5 g of a material with density 8.0 g/mL:
3.5 g × (1 mL / 8.0 g) = 0.44 mL

Show/Hide Example: Volume to Mass

Given 25.0 mL of a fluid with density 1.50 g/mL:
25.0 mL × (1.50 g / 1 mL) = 37.5 g
