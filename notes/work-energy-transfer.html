Work

Work is the transfer of energy by applying a force over a distance. If the force is constant:

Only the component of force parallel to the displacement does work:
\[ F_{//} = F \cos\theta \]
Work equation:
\[ W = Fd\cos\theta \]
(where d is displacement, θ is the angle between force and displacement)

Show/Hide Examples

Example 1: A student applies a 200 N force horizontally to a box for 3.0 m.
\[ W = (200\,\text{N})(3.0\,\text{m}) = 600\,\text{J} \]

Example 2: A 200 N force at 30° below horizontal for 3.0 m.
\[ W = (200\,\text{N})\cos(30^\circ)(3.0\,\text{m}) = 520\,\text{J} \]

Only the force component in the direction of motion does work. The mass of the object is not needed unless you are asked about energy or acceleration.

Work vs Net Work

When multiple forces act, be clear if you need the work done by one force or the net work (total energy change).

Show/Hide Example

Example: A 200 N force pushes a 30 kg box 5 m; friction is 25 N.
a) Work by student: \[ W = (200\,\text{N})(5\,\text{m}) = 1000\,\text{J} \]
b) Work by ground (friction): \[ W = (25\,\text{N})(5\,\text{m}) = 125\,\text{J} \]
c) Net work: \[ 1000\,\text{J} - 125\,\text{J} = 875\,\text{J} \]
d) Final speed: \[ W = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \] \[ 875 = 0.5 \times 30 \times v^2 \] \[ v = 7.6\,\text{m/s} \]

Conservation of energy can be used even when net force is not constant, unlike kinematics.

Putting It All Together

Show/Hide Example

Example: A 5.0 kg box starts at rest. A 40 N force pushes it up a 6.0 m hill (distance traveled: 15 m). What is its speed at the top? (Neglect friction.)

Energy equation: 0+W=Ug+K 0 + W = U_g + K (40)(15)=(5)(10)(6)+0.5(5)v2 (40)(15) = (5)(10)(6) + 0.5(5)v^2 600=300+2.5v2 600 = 300 + 2.5v^2 v=11m/s v = 11\,\text{m/s}
