Sound Waves

Sound waves are longitudinal waves. When two objects impact, they push nearby air particles, which push other air particles, and so on, until the vibration reaches your ear and is interpreted as sound.

No sound in a vacuum: Sound needs a medium (like air) to travel. Space battles in movies would actually be silent!

How do we hear astronauts? Their voices vibrate air, which vibrates a microphone. The microphone converts this to an electrical signal, which is sent as a transverse electromagnetic wave (radio) to Earth, where it is converted back to sound by a speaker.

Can-phone demo: Two cans connected by a tight string transmit sound as a combination of longitudinal (air) and transverse (string) waves.

Pitch

Our brains interpret the frequency of sound waves as pitch. High frequency = high pitch (Mickey Mouse), low frequency = low pitch (Darth Vader).

Sound waves are often drawn as transverse waves for simplicity, but remember: sound is a longitudinal pressure wave.

Human hearing range: ~20 Hz to 20,000 Hz (upper range decreases with age).

Loudness

Loudness is related to the intensity of a sound wave, which depends on its amplitude. Larger amplitude = louder sound.

Amplifiers increase amplitude to make sounds louder (think electric guitar).

Human hearing intensity range: from \(1 \times 10^{-12}\) W/m² (barely audible) to \(1 \times 10^4\) W/m² (painful).

Decibel scale: A logarithmic scale for sound intensity. Doubling intensity does not double perceived loudness.

Timbre

Timbre is what makes a clarinet, trumpet, and violin sound different even if they play the same note at the same volume. Most sounds are a mix of a fundamental frequency and several harmonics.

Example: Middle C (262 Hz) on a piano also produces harmonics at 524 Hz and 786 Hz (2× and 3× the fundamental).

The superposition of these frequencies creates a unique waveform (not a perfect sine wave). Each instrument has a unique set of harmonics, giving it a unique timbre.

Tuning forks produce almost pure tones (single frequency). Music synthesizers mimic harmonic patterns to recreate timbre. Voice recognition analyzes harmonics to identify speakers.

Beat Frequency

When two sound waves of similar frequency combine, they create a pattern of alternating loud and soft sounds called beats.

The beat frequency is the number of beats per second, equal to the difference in frequencies:
fbeat=|f1−f2| f_{\text{beat}} = |f_1 - f_2|

Example: A tuner emits 262 Hz, a violin emits 265 Hz.
fbeat=|265−262|=3Hz f_{\text{beat}} = |265 - 262| = 3\,\text{Hz}
The student hears 3 loud-soft pulses per second.
