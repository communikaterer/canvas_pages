Power in Circuits

Power is the rate at which energy is transferred in or out of a system:

\[ P = \frac{W}{t} \]

- P is power (watts, W)
- W is work (energy, joules)
- t is time (seconds)

In circuits, power can also be calculated as:

\[ P = IV \]

Where I is current (amps) and V is voltage (volts).

Using Ohm's Law ($V = IR$), we get other forms:

\[ P = IV = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R} \]

Series: Same current, so $P \propto R$ (higher resistance = more power).
Parallel: Same voltage, so $P \propto \frac{1}{R}$ (lower resistance = more power).

Example 1: Bulb Brightness
Houses in the US are wired to provide 120 V. Which bulb is brighter: one with 144 Ω or one with 240 Ω?
Show/Hide Answer

The brightest bulb emits the most energy per second (highest power).
Both bulbs have the same voltage, so use: \[ P = \frac{V^2}{R} \]
For 144 Ω: \[ P = \frac{(120)^2}{144} = 100\,\text{W} \]
For 240 Ω: \[ P = \frac{(120)^2}{240} = 60\,\text{W} \]
The 144 Ω bulb is brighter.

Example 2: Series Circuit Power
A student wires a 3 Ω resistor in series with a 10 Ω resistor and a 9 V battery. What is the power rating for each resistor and the total circuit?
Show/Hide Answer

Step 1: Find total resistance
\[ R = 3 + 10 = 13\,\Omega \]
Step 2: Find current
\[ V = IR \implies I = \frac{9}{13} = 0.69\,\text{A} \]
Step 3: Find total power
\[ P = IV = 0.69 \times 9 = 6.2\,\text{W} \]
Step 4: Find power for each resistor
\[ P = I^2R \]
For 3 Ω: \[ P = (0.69)^2 \times 3 = 1.43\,\text{W} \]
For 10 Ω: \[ P = (0.69)^2 \times 10 = 4.76\,\text{W} \]
\[ 1.43 + 4.76 = 6.2\,\text{W} \]
The sum of the power ratings equals the total circuit power.
