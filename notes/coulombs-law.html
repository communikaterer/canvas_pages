Coulomb's Law

We've discussed the presence of electrostatic forces: two objects with the same type of net charge will repel while two objects with different types of net charge will attract. Extensive experimentation has shown that this static electric force is:

  • Proportional to the quantity of net charge on both objects
  • Proportional to the inverse-square of the distance between the two charged objects

In other words:

\[F_e \propto \frac{q_1q_2}{r^2}\]

\[F_e\]
electrostatic force,
\[q_1, q_2\]
net charge of objects 1 and 2,
\[r\]
distance between the two objects

Example 1: Distance Effect

The distance d between two positively charged particles is measured. The distance between the two particles is increased to 2d. If the original electrostatic force between the objects was F, what is the new force?

Click to Show/Hide Solution

Force has an inverse-squared relationship with distance. If the distance is doubled then the force will change by a factor of: \[(\frac{1}{2})^2 = \frac{1}{4}\] In other words, the new force will be four times smaller.

Since the two particles are both positive, the force will be a repelling force.

In order to turn this relationship into a full equation, a constant of proportionality must be added. This constant is called the coulomb's law constant and has a value of \[9 \times 10^9 \frac{\text{ N-m}^2}{\text{C}^2}\].

This equation should look a little familiar ‚Äì it's the same format as the universal law of gravitation.

Example 2: Force Calculation

What is the static electric force between a -3.0 ŒºC charge and a +6 ŒºC charge if they are 2.0 cm apart? Also, which is larger, the force of the negative particle on the positive or the force of the positive particle on the negative?

Click to Show/Hide Solution

\[F = \frac{k \cdot q_1q_2}{r^2}\]

\[F = \frac{(9 \times 10^9 \frac{\text{ N-m}^2}{\text{C}^2})(3 \times 10^{-6} \text{ C})(6 \times 10^{-6} \text{ C})}{(0.02 \text{ m})^2}\]

\[F = 405 \text{ N}\]

Remember that the signs on the charges have no mathematical meaning so they should not be included in the math. The different types of net charge does mean that the force is an attractive force.

Newton's third law notes that any two objects pushing or pulling on each other will push/pull with equal but opposite forces. Each applies the same 405 N of attractive force on the other.

Multiple Particle Analysis

What if we have more than two charged particles? The same as we've always done:

  1. Sketch the system if a drawing is not provided
  2. Draw and label a free body diagram
  3. Calculate all the forces
  4. Solve for any x and y force components
  5. Add all the x direction forces together and then add all the y direction forces together
  6. Use the Pythagorean theorem to combine the components into their vector form
  7. Use arctangent to solve for the direction of the final vector form

Example 3: Three Particles

Particle A has a net charge of +4 mC and is located at coordinate position (0, 2) m. Particle B has a net charge of -2 mC and is located at coordinate position (0, 0) m. Particle C has a net charge of +6 mC and is located at position (3, 0) m. What is the net force on particle B? Ignore any gravity effects.

Click to Show/Hide Solution

First, let's look at the system diagram and free body diagram:

Calculate the forces:

\[F = \frac{k \cdot q_1 \cdot q_2}{r^2}\]

\[F_{A \text{ on } B} = \frac{(9 \times 10^9 \frac{\text{ N-m}^2}{\text{C}^2})(4 \times 10^{-3} \text{ C})(2 \times 10^{-3} \text{ C})}{(2 \text{ m})^2}\]

\[F_{A \text{ on } B} = 18000 \text{ N}\]

\[F_{C \text{ on } B} = \frac{(9 \times 10^9 \frac{\text{ N-m}^2}{\text{C}^2})(6 \times 10^{-3} \text{ C})(2 \times 10^{-3} \text{ C})}{(3 \text{ m})^2}\]

\[F_{C \text{ on } B} = 12000 \text{ N}\]

Find the net force magnitude:

\[\Sigma F^2 = F_y^2 + F_x^2\]

\[(\Sigma F)^2 = (18000\text{ N})^2 + (12000\text{ N})^2\]

\[\Sigma F = 21633\text{ N}\]

Find the direction:

\[\tan \theta = \frac{18000\text{ N}}{12000\text{ N}}\]

\[\theta = 56.3¬∞\]

Example 4: Four Particles

Particles A and B have charges of +6 ŒºC and are located at positions (0, 4) m and (4, 4) m, respectively. Particles C and D have charges of -6 ŒºC and are located at positions (0, 0) m and (4, 0) m, respectively. Solve for the net force on particle C.

Show to Show/Hide Solution

System diagram and free body diagram:

Calculate individual forces:

\[F_e = \frac{k \cdot q_1\cdot q_2}{r^2}\]

\[F_{A \text{ on } C} = \frac{(9 \times 10^9 \frac{\text{ N-m}^2}{\text{C}^2})(6 \times 10^{-6} \text{ C})(6 \times 10^{-6} \text{ C})}{ (4 \text{ m})^2}\]

\[F_{A \text{ on } C} = 0.0203 \text{ N}\]

\[F_{B \text{ on } C} = \frac{(9 \times 10^9 \text{ N-m}^2\text{/C}^2)(6 \times 10^{-6} \text{ C})(6 \times 10^{-6} \text{ C})}{(5.66 \text{ m})^2}\]

\[F_{B \text{ on } C} = 0.0101 \text{ N}\]

Component analysis:

\[F_x = (0.0101 \text{ N})(\cos 45¬∞) = 0.00716 \text{ N}\]

\[F_x = (0.00716 \text{ N}) + (-0.0203 \text{ N}) = -0.0131 \text{ N}\]

\[\Sigma F_y = (0.00716 \text{ N}) + (0.0203 \text{ N}) = 0.0275 \text{ N}\]

Net force magnitude:

\[\Sigma F^2 = (0.0275\text{ N})^2 + (-0.0131\text{ N})^2\]

\[\Sigma F = 0.0305\text{ N}\]

Direction:

\[\tan \theta = \frac{0.0275\text{ N}}{-0.0131\text{ N}}\]

\[\theta = 115.5¬∞\]
