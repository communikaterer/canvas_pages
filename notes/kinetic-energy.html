Summary of Results

From the class data, we can see that kinetic energy is proportional to speed squared. This means that the original kinetic energy vs speed graph forms an upward opening parabola, implying a $y$ vs $x^2$ (or in this case, $E_K$ vs $v^2$) relationship.

We can test this relationship by squaring all of the velocity data and then creating a graph of $E_K$ vs $v^2$. For most students, the test graph formed a straight line. This means that the $E_K$ vs $v^2$ relationship is correct. We can then use the linear test graph to create the final equation for the lab data.

Upward parabolas have the general form:
\[ y = mx^2 + b \]
where $m$ and $b$ are the slope and y-intercept from the linear test graph.


Significance of y-intercept and slope


Y-intercept: The y-intercept is the amount of kinetic energy when the object's speed is zero. Since kinetic energy is defined as the energy stored in an object's motion, zero speed means zero kinetic energy. The y-intercept should be zero by definition! If you get a significant y-intercept, this indicates significant error in the data.

Slope: The units of the slope are J/(m/s)2. Breaking this down:
1 Joule = 1 N·m = 1 (kg·m/s2)·m = kg·m2/s2
So, slope units: (kg·m2/s2)/(m2/s2) = kg

This tells us the slope is related to mass. In most cases, the slope value is about half the mass of the object. The slope of the linear test graph represents half the mass of the moving object.

Kinetic Energy Equation

The final equation should take the form: $ y = mx^2 + b$
For kinetic energy:
$$ E_K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 $$
where $E_K$ is kinetic energy, $m$ is mass, and $v$ is speed.

Examples

Example 1
An object moving at 4 m/s has 300 J of kinetic energy. What is the kinetic energy when the object's speed is 8 m/s?

Show/Hide Answer

Since kinetic energy is proportional to speed squared, doubling the speed means four times more kinetic energy.
\[ (300\,\text{J}) \times 4 = 1200\,\text{J} \]
When this object has a speed of 8 m/s, it has a kinetic energy of 1200 J.

Example 2

What is the kinetic energy stored in a 60 kg student running at a speed of 2 m/s?

Show/Hide Answer

\[ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \]
\[ K = \frac{1}{2} (60\,\text{kg})(2\,\text{m/s})^2 \]
\[ K = 120\,\text{J} \]

Example 3

A student drops a 25 kg box from a height of 8.0 m. What is the speed of the box just before it hits the ground? (Neglect air resistance.)

Show/Hide Answer

Use conservation of energy:
\[ U_g = K \]
\[ mgy = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \]
Mass cancels:
\[ gy = \frac{1}{2}v^2 \]
\[ v^2 = 2gy \]
\[ v^2 = 2(10\,\text{N/kg})(8.0\,\text{m}) \]
\[ v = 12.6\,\text{m/s} \]
The speed is 12.6 m/s. The velocity would be -12.6 m/s since the box is moving downward.

Example: Spring and Box

A student pushes a 0.5 kg box up against a spring (k = 100 N/m) compressing the spring by 0.10 m in the horizontal direction. What is the final speed of the box once the student lets go? (Assume a frictionless surface.)

Show/Hide Answer

All of the mechanical energy is stored in the spring at the start. All of the energy is transferred to the box as kinetic energy.
Us=K U_s = K
12kΔx2=12mv2 \frac{1}{2}k\Delta x^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2
12(100N/m)(0.10m)2=12(0.5kg)v2 \frac{1}{2}(100\,\text{N/m})(0.10\,\text{m})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.5\,\text{kg})v^2
v=1.4m/s v = 1.4\,\text{m/s}
