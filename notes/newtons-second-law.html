Newton's Second Law

Experimentation has shown that the net force acting on an object is directly proportional to the resulting acceleration. If you double the net force, you double the acceleration:

\[ \Sigma F \propto a \]

To turn this into an equation, we need a constant. That constant is the object's inertia (mass):

\[ \Sigma F = ma \]

Where ΣF is the sum of forces, m is mass, and a is acceleration. The acceleration is always in the same direction as the net force.

Example 1:
A student pushes a 15 kg box with a horizontal force of 20 N. The friction force on the box is 10 N. What is the normal force on the box? What is the acceleration of the box?
Show/Hide Answer

Step 1: Draw and label the force diagram

Step 2: Find the normal force (vertical direction)
The box is not accelerating vertically, so vertical forces must cancel:
\[ \Sigma F_y = 0 \] \[ N + W = 0 \] \[ N + (-147) = 0 \] \[ N = 147\,\text{N} \]
Step 3: Find the acceleration (horizontal direction)
The box is accelerating horizontally:
\[ \Sigma F = ma \] \[ F_{student} + f = ma \] \[ 20 + (-10) = 15a \] \[ 10 = 15a \] \[ a = 0.67\,\text{m/s}^2 \]
The normal force is 147 N. The acceleration is 0.67 m/s².

Example 2:
A 60 kg student accelerates upwards in an elevator at a rate of 2.0 m/s². What is the normal force on the student?
Show/Hide Answer

Step 1: Draw and label the force diagram

Step 2: Set up Newton's Second Law (vertical)
ΣFy=ma \Sigma F_y = ma \[ N + W = ma \] \[ N + (-588) = 60 \times 2.0 \] \[ N - 588 = 120 \] \[ N = 708\,\text{N} \]
The normal force is 708 N.
This makes sense: the normal force must be greater than the student's weight to accelerate upward.

Gravity, Acceleration, and Units

Newton's Second Law also explains why the acceleration due to gravity (gg) has the same value as the gravitational field strength.

  • Drop a ball: only force is gravity (WW).
  • ΣFy=ma \Sigma F_y = ma
  • W=ma W = ma
  • But W=mg W = mg
  • So mg=ma mg = ma
  • g=a g = a

If the only force acting on a falling object is gravity, its acceleration equals the gravitational field strength of the planet.

The units for gravitational field strength (N/kgN/kg) are the same as for acceleration (m/s2m/s^2), because:
1N=1kg⋅m/s2 1\,N = 1\,kg \cdot m/s^2
Nkg=kg⋅m/s2kg=m/s2 \frac{N}{kg} = \frac{kg \cdot m/s^2}{kg} = m/s^2
