Friction

Friction is a force that opposes the motion (or attempted motion) of two surfaces in contact. It acts parallel to the surfaces and opposite to the direction of motion.

Friction is caused by microscopic bumps and chemical interactions between surfaces.

Types of Friction

  • Static friction: Prevents motion when an object is at rest. It must be overcome to start moving an object.
  • Kinetic (sliding) friction: Acts when an object is already moving.
  • Rolling friction: Acts when an object rolls over a surface (usually much less than sliding friction).

Friction Calculations

The force of friction is proportional to the normal force (the force perpendicular to the surfaces in contact):
f=μN f = \mu N
where f is the friction force, μ is the coefficient of friction (depends on the materials), and N is the normal force.

  • Static friction: fs≤μsN f_s \leq \mu_s N
  • Kinetic friction: fk=μkN f_k = \mu_k N

Usually, μs>μk \mu_s > \mu_k (it takes more force to start moving than to keep moving).

Example: A 10 kg box is pushed across a floor with a coefficient of kinetic friction μ = 0.2. What is the friction force?
Step 1: Find the normal force: N=mg=10kg×9.8N/kg=98N N = mg = 10\,\text{kg} \times 9.8\,\text{N/kg} = 98\,\text{N}
Step 2: Calculate friction force: fk=μkN=0.2×98=19.6N f_k = \mu_k N = 0.2 \times 98 = 19.6\,\text{N}
