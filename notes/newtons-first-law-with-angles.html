Example 1: Pushing with a Downward Angle

A student applies a force of 200 N at an angle of 30° below the horizontal to a 25 kg box, causing it to slide at a constant 2.5 m/s. What is the normal force? What is the friction force?
Show/Hide Answer
Step 1: Draw and label the force diagram.
Step 2: Resolve the student's force into components:
\[ F_{y,\text{student}} = 200\sin 30^\circ = -100\,\text{N} \]
(Negative because it's downward.)
\[ F_{x,\text{student}} = 200\cos 30^\circ = 173\,\text{N} \]
Step 3: Find the normal force (vertical direction):
\[ \Sigma F_y = 0 \] \[ N + W + F_{y,\text{student}} = 0 \] \[ N + (-245) + (-100) = 0 \] \[ N = 345\,\text{N} \]
Step 4: Find the friction force (horizontal direction):
\[ \Sigma F_x = 0 \] \[ f + F_{x,\text{student}} = 0 \] \[ f + 173 = 0 \] \[ f = -173\,\text{N} \]
The normal force is 345 N. The friction force is 173 N to the left.

Example 2: Pulling with an Upward Angle

A student pulls a 200 kg desk along the floor with a force of 200 N at an angle of 30° above the horizontal. The desk moves at constant velocity. What is the normal force? What is the friction force?
Show/Hide Answer

Step 1: Resolve the student's force:
\[ F_{y,\text{student}} = 200\sin 30^\circ = 100\,\text{N} \]
\[ F_{x,\text{student}} = 200\cos 30^\circ = 173\,\text{N} \]
Step 2: Find the normal force (vertical direction):
\[ \Sigma F_y = 0 \] \[ N + F_{y,\text{student}} + W = 0 \] \[ N + 100 + (-1960) = 0 \] \[ N = 1860\,\text{N} \]
Step 3: Find the friction force (horizontal direction):
\[ \Sigma F_x = 0 \] \[ f + F_{x,\text{student}} = 0 \] \[ f + 173 = 0 \] \[ f = -173\,\text{N} \]
The normal force is 1860 N. The friction force is 173 N to the left.

Example 3: Tension in Ropes

A student suspends a 5.0 kg box from several ropes as shown. The tension in the horizontal rope is -30 N. What is the magnitude of the tension in the rope connected to the ceiling?
Show/Hide Answer

Step 1: Draw and label the force diagram.
Step 2: Find components of T2:
- Horizontal: T1=30N T_1 = 30\,\text{N} - Vertical: W=5.0×9.8=49N W = 5.0 \times 9.8 = 49\,\text{N}
Step 3: Use Pythagorean theorem:
(30)2+(49)2=(T2)2 (30)^2 + (49)^2 = (T_2)^2 T2=57.5N T_2 = 57.5\,\text{N}
If the ceiling rope can't supply at least 57.5 N, it will break and the box will fall.
