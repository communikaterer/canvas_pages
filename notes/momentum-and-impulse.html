Momentum

Momentum is another of those annoying physics concepts that is defined by an equation. There aren’t many good ways of conceptually defining momentum. We’re going to try anyway. One way to think about momentum is as a measurement of how difficult it’s going to be to stop a particular moving object. 
We know that if an object has a lot of inertia (measured by mass) that it will be difficult to change the motion of the object. This means inertia probably has something to do with momentum.  
We also know that if an object is moving with a high velocity that it will also be difficult to stop. We’d either have to apply a small net force for a long time or a large net force for a small time. This means velocity probably has something to do with momentum.   Momentum depends on both mass (inertia) and velocity:

\[ p = mv \]

Where p is momentum, m is mass, and v is velocity. Units: kg·m/s. Momentum is a vector (same direction as velocity).

Change in momentum:
\[ \Delta p = p_{final} - p_{initial} \]
For constant mass: \[ \Delta p = m\Delta v \]

Example 1:
Sphere A has a mass of 2.0 kg and moves at 5 m/s. Sphere B has a mass of 1.0 kg and moves at 7 m/s. Which sphere has more momentum?
Show/Hide Answer

\[ p_A = (2.0\,\text{kg})(5\,\text{m/s}) = 10\,\text{kg·m/s} \]
\[ p_B = (1.0\,\text{kg})(7\,\text{m/s}) = 7\,\text{kg·m/s} \]
Sphere A has more momentum.

Example 2:
A 5.0 kg box moving at 3.0 m/s slows to 1.0 m/s due to friction. What was the box’s change in momentum?
Note: For constant mass, you can use \[ \Delta p = m\Delta v \].
Show/Hide Answer

\[ \Delta p = (5.0\,\text{kg})(1.0\,\text{m/s}) - (5.0\,\text{kg})(3.0\,\text{m/s}) = -10\,\text{kg·m/s} \]

In this course, we only consider constant mass systems.

Impulse

Impulse is the product of force and time. It measures how much effort is put into making something speed up or slow down.

\[ J = Ft \]

Where J is impulse, F is force, t is time. Units: N·s.
For multiple forces: \[ \Sigma J = \Sigma Ft \]

Example 3:
A 20 kg rocket experiences an engine thrust of 300 N for 5.0 s. What is the impulse provided by the engine? What is the net impulse on the rocket?
The net impulse determines the rocket’s motion.
Show/Hide Answer

\[ J_{engine} = (300\,\text{N})(5.0\,\text{s}) = 1500\,\text{N·s} \]
\[ \Sigma J = (300\,\text{N} - 196\,\text{N})(5.0\,\text{s}) = 520\,\text{N·s} \]

Impulse-Momentum Theorem

Newton’s Second Law: \[ \Sigma F = ma \]
Acceleration: \[ a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \]
\[ \Sigma F = m\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \]
\[ \Delta p = m\Delta v \]
\[ \Sigma F = \frac{\Delta p}{t} \]
Rearranged: \[ \Sigma Ft = m\Delta v \]
Net impulse equals change in momentum. This is the impulse-momentum theorem.

Especially useful for analyzing collisions.

Example 4:
A 0.145 kg baseball approaches a batter at 30 m/s. The batter hits the ball and it leaves in the opposite direction at 20 m/s. If the ball and bat were in contact for 0.01 s, what was the force of the bat on the ball?
Show/Hide Answer

\[ \Delta v = -20\,\text{m/s} - 30\,\text{m/s} = -50\,\text{m/s} \]
\[ \Sigma F t = m\Delta v \]
\[ \Sigma F (0.01\,\text{s}) = (0.145\,\text{kg})(-50\,\text{m/s}) \]
\[ \Sigma F = -725\,\text{N} \]

The net force (from the bat) is -725 N.
Example 5:
A 30 kg rocket is initially at rest. The rocket experiences an upward 400 N of force for 6.0 s. What is the final speed of the rocket? (Neglect fuel mass.)
Model rocket engines are rated by impulse and burn time. The engine thrust must be greater than the rocket's weight for launch.
Show/Hide Answer

\[ (400 - 294)(6.0) = 30(v_{final}) \]
\[ v_{final} = 21.2\,\text{m/s} \]

Example 6:
Class B model rocket engines can apply 5.0 N·s of impulse. If a class B engine with a burn time of 1.2 s is attached to a 0.15 kg rocket, what maximum speed will the rocket reach? (Ignore air resistance.)
Show/Hide Answer

\[ F = \frac{5.0}{1.2} = 4.2\,\text{N} \]
\[ ((4.2) - (0.15)(9.8))(1.2) = 0.15(\Delta v) \]
\[ \Delta v = 21.8\,\text{m/s} \]

Example 7:
A 0.02 kg ball falling at 10 m/s bounces off the ground. The final speed is 5 m/s upwards. If the ball was in contact with the ground for 0.003 s, what was the normal force on the ball?
Show/Hide Answer

\[ (N - 0.196)(0.003) = 0.02(10 - (-5)) \]
\[ N = 100\,\text{N} \]

Example 8:
In the original Superman movie, Lois Lane falls off a skyscraper and is caught by Superman, going from falling to moving upward in less than one second. In terms of momentum and impulse, explain why this is not good for Lois.
Show/Hide Answer

Lois’s change in velocity is large (from large downward to upward). Since this occurs over a very short time, the force is extremely large, which is not good for Lois.

Estimate: \[ v^2 = 2a\Delta y \], \[ v \approx -49\,\text{m/s} \].
Assume final upward speed 2.2 m/s, \[ \Delta v \approx 50\,\text{m/s} \], mass ≈ 54 kg.
\[ (F_{superman} - 529)(1.0) = 2700 \]
\[ F \approx 3229\,\text{N} \]
This is a very large force, likely to cause injury.
