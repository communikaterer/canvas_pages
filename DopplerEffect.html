Stationary Sound Source

Consider a stationary speaker emitting sound waves in all directions. These longitudinal pressure waves spread out uniformly, similar to ripples in a pond when dropping rocks.

Key Point: When the source is stationary, all observers hear the same frequency (pitch), regardless of their position.

The Doppler Effect

When the source moves while emitting waves, an interesting phenomenon occurs:

Wave Behavior:

  • Waves compress in front of the moving source
  • Waves stretch out behind the source
  • Similar to a boat's wake in water

Animation from ophysics.com

Observer Effects:

  • Observer ahead of source: Higher frequency (higher pitch)
  • Observer behind source: Lower frequency (lower pitch)
  • Example: Car engine sound changing as it passes by ("neeaaarroooom")

Applications Beyond Sound:

The Doppler effect applies to all waves, including light:

  • "Red shift": Observed frequencies lower than emitted
  • "Blue shift": Observed frequencies higher than emitted
  • Used to detect universe expansion through star observation

Sonic Booms

The speed of sound varies with conditions but is approximately:

$$v_{sound} = 340 \text{ m/s}$$

Sonic Boom Formation:

  • Occurs when source moves faster than sound speed
  • Sound waves overlap and superimpose
  • Creates multiple boom events, not just one
  • Results in significantly larger wave amplitudes (louder sound)
